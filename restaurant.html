BiteBuddy

BiteBuddy, Restaurant App

1st Semester Design Sprint, Digital Concept Development


Task: Develop a digital solution to improve the dining experience for people with food allergies while ensuring the solution is easy to use for both customers and restaurant staff.

Our challenge: "How can we ensure that our solution makes life easier and doesn’t add extra work for both users and restaurants?"

We tackled this challenge using a Design Sprint, rapidly ideating, prototyping, and testing a concept within a short timeframe.

This was a group project, and on Day 1, I took on the role of group facilitator, ensuring a structured discussion as we aligned on user needs and problem definitions.

Process

1. Map and Sketch, Days 1-2

As group facilitator on Day 1, I guided the team through research synthesis and problem definition exercises. Our research included expert interviews, user insights, and competitor analysis. I also facilitated the experience mapping process, ensuring we captured key user journeys and identified pain points to address.The main pain points we identified were:

  • Poor communication between guests, waiters, and the kitchen.
  • Human error in handling allergy information.
  • Limited menu options.

We identified three key goals we wanted to address in the solution:

  • Decrease mistakes in allergy communication.
  • Make dining out easier for people with allergies.
  • Focus on an easy to use self-service solution.

On day 2, we did sketching. Each team member rapidly generated ideas using the Crazy 8s method, before working on a more detailed solution sketch.

2. Define and Prototype, Days 3-4

Next, we collaboratively voted on key features for our prototype. These included:

  • User Allergy Profile – Customers input allergies and intolerances.
  • Smart Menu Scanning – Users scan a QR code to see a menu tailored to their allergies.
  • Full & Compatible Menus – A choice between a "safe" menu or the full menu with conflict items clearly marked.
  • Ingredient Substitutions – Users swap out conflicting ingredients where possible.
  • Order Confirmation – Allergy information is automatically sent to the kitchen.

On day 4, we concentrated on prototyping the app in Figma.

3. Testing and Iterations, Day 5

We conducted user testing using the Think Aloud method with four participants to validate our concept. Key testing questions included:

  • Would the app be valuable for managing allergies?
  • How comfortable were users with ingredient substitutions?
  • Did they trust the app to communicate their needs to the kitchen?

Some key takeaways from the testing included:

  • Menu visibility – 3/4 users preferred to see the full menu first, with an option to filter for compatible items.
  • Trust & confidence – All users said they're trust the app, and 2/4 users said they’d trust the app more than a waiter, as it removes human error.
  • Ingredient substitution clarity – 2/4 users found it unclear that conflict items could be swapped; better onboarding or wording is needed.

Future Considerations

1. Additional Refinements

Based on the feedback we received during testing, we'd like to refine the app by doing the following:

  • Improve how we show conflict items – Keep the red warning color but make substitutes clearer.
  • Menu filtering options – Re-evaluate whether to show the full menu first or add filtering options.
  • Usability tweaks – Add an info icon to guide new users.

2. Real-World Implementation

During a written exam following the Design Sprint, I evaluated BiteBuddy using the Desirability, Feasibility, and Viability framework.

  • Desirability: Testing showed strong interest in the solution. Most users preferred the app over speaking to a waiter, though some might still prefer human interaction.
  • Feasibility: The technology exists (QR menus, digital ordering), but integrating with restaurant POS systems could be complex. In addition, restaurants would need to input ingredient data and keep it updated, which could be a challenge.
  • Viability: Larger chains may see value in improving allergy management and reducing liability. Independent restaurants may hesitate due to setup costs. A freemium model or POS integration partnerships could make it more viable.

3. Final Thoughts

This project was a great opportunity to apply UX principles in a fast-paced, structured design process. As group facilitator on Day 1, I helped ensure a strong foundation by focusing on problem definition before moving to solutions.

While the sprint enabled rapid ideation and iteration, real-world implementation would require further research into restaurant adoption and integration challenges.
